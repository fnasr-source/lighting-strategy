# Admireworks Copy Frameworks

This file is the canonical copy doctrine for new client projects.

Use it with:
- `docs/messaging/ADMIREWORKS_COPY_METHOD.md`
- `docs/messaging/COPY_REVIEW_RUBRIC.md`
- approved source material in `docs/client_kb/`

## Core Rule
Copy must be built from real client context, market language, and proof.
Frameworks shape the work, but source material controls the truth.

## Admireworks Copy Method
Every serious copy task should move through this sequence:
1. source extraction
2. market-language capture
3. StoryBrand narrative spine
4. offer logic
5. proof mapping
6. objection mapping
7. CTA ladder selection
8. channel adaptation
9. review against the rubric

Do not skip steps because a framework feels familiar.

## 1. Strategic Foundation
### StoryBrand
Every campaign should be legible through:
- Character
- Problem
- Guide
- Plan
- CTA
- Success
- Failure

Use this as the narrative spine for:
- strategy documents
- landing pages
- campaign flows
- sales pages
- webinar pages
- sales scripts

### Direct-Response Overlay
StoryBrand gives the narrative spine.
Direct response gives the conversion pressure.

Every execution asset should also answer:
- why this matters now
- why this offer is worth acting on
- why the buyer should trust it
- what action should happen next

## 2. Source-First Writing Rules
Before drafting, extract and separate:
- confirmed facts
- inferred positioning
- open questions
- direct customer language
- direct founder language
- proof assets
- recurring objections

Do not turn assumptions into claims.
Do not write certainty where the source only supports possibility.

## 3. One-Customer Rule
Do not write for "everyone."
Choose one primary buyer for each asset and define:
- role or identity
- current frustration
- desired outcome
- emotional stakes
- buying hesitation
- level of awareness

If multiple segments exist, split the copy by segment instead of averaging the language.

## 4. Offer Writing Rules
Every offer should clearly state:
- who it is for
- what painful problem it solves
- what concrete outcome it creates
- what makes it different
- what is included
- what proof supports it
- what objection handling exists
- what risk reversal exists
- what urgency mechanism exists
- what action must happen now

Offer writing should answer "why this offer, from this guide, in this moment."

## 5. Proof Mapping Rules
Every important claim should map to proof.

Proof can include:
- testimonials
- specific stories
- quantified outcomes
- credentials
- process evidence
- screenshots
- before/after contrasts
- market evidence
- founder experience

Tie each proof point to the claim it supports.
If a claim has no proof, either weaken the claim or flag it for confirmation.

## 6. Objection Handling Rules
Address the likely blockers explicitly:
- time
- money
- trust
- complexity
- fit
- fear of failure
- skepticism from past bad experiences
- "this will not work for me"

Good objection handling reduces friction without sounding defensive.
It should sound like a strong guide anticipating real hesitation.

## 7. CTA Ladder Rules
A CTA should be:
- clear
- singular
- easy to understand
- aligned with stage of awareness
- consistent with channel intent

Use stage logic:
- cold audience: softer discovery CTA
- problem-aware audience: proof-backed CTA
- solution-aware audience: stronger booking or purchase CTA
- hesitant audience: lower-friction CTA with clear next step

Do not force a hard CTA where trust has not been earned.

## 8. Landing Page Framework
Default conversion sequence:
1. Hero
2. Problem
3. Agitation / stakes
4. Guide / authority
5. Plan / mechanism
6. Offer stack
7. Proof
8. Objection handling
9. Risk reversal
10. Urgency / scarcity
11. FAQ
12. Final CTA

For high-trust or high-ticket offers, add more proof and process clarity before pressure.
For simpler offers, compress the middle sections but keep the logic intact.

## 9. Ad Copy Frameworks
Use frameworks deliberately, not randomly.

### PAS
Use for pain-led ads.
- Problem
- Agitation
- Solution

### Before / After / Bridge
Use when the transformation is vivid and believable.
- before state
- after state
- bridge to get there

### Hook / Story / Offer / CTA
Use for founder-led, narrative, or voice-heavy ads.

### Lead / Proof / Offer / CTA
Use when trust and evidence matter more than novelty.

### Problem / Proof / Plan / CTA
Use when the buyer needs clarity and confidence more than emotion alone.

## 10. Ad Angle System
Every ad set should be organized by angle, not just by wording variation.

Common angle types:
- pain aware
- outcome aware
- myth busting
- founder authority
- mechanism / how it works
- objection handling
- urgency
- proof / testimonial
- comparison / category reframe
- risk-reversal

Each angle should have:
- one core tension
- one belief it tries to shift
- one dominant proof pattern
- one CTA direction

## 11. Hook Rules
Hooks should usually do one of these:
- name the pain sharply
- expose the hidden cause
- create curiosity around the mechanism
- challenge a wrong belief
- promise a specific desirable shift
- reframe a familiar frustration in sharper language

Hooks should avoid vague motivation language and empty intensity.

## 12. Market Language Rules
Customer-facing copy should sound native to the market.
Avoid literal translation energy.

Prefer:
- local phrasing
- concrete language
- emotional precision
- short high-impact lines where useful
- real buyer vocabulary from calls, chats, and sales notes

### Arabic
- write as a native market writer, not as translated English
- choose the right register for the market: Gulf, Egyptian, MSA-heavy, or mixed
- keep CTA wording natural and commercially clear
- avoid formal stiffness unless the category requires it

### English
- avoid startup cliches, inflated adjectives, and agency filler
- favor commercially sharp, sentence-driven writing over slogan noise
- keep claims grounded and easy to picture

If the copy could be swapped into any generic agency page, it is not specific enough.

## 13. WhatsApp And Email Rules
These channels should feel more personal and direct than landing pages.
They still need:
- one clear objective per message
- friction-aware CTA
- continuity with the main campaign promise
- proper timing and sequence logic
- clear handoff between steps

WhatsApp should usually feel conversational, low-friction, and context-aware.
Email can carry more structure, proof, and argument.

## 14. Anti-AI Guardrails
Do not ship copy that:
- sounds translated
- stacks generic claims without evidence
- uses motivational filler
- overuses adjectives instead of specifics
- imitates "viral copy" cadence without business fit
- treats every market like a SaaS launch page

High-risk phrases to replace:
- "unlock your potential"
- "take your business to the next level"
- "revolutionary solution"
- "game-changer"
- "seamless experience"
- "tailored excellence"

The fix is usually not "better adjectives."
The fix is better truth, sharper stakes, and stronger proof.

## 15. Human Quality Standard
The final copy should read like it came from:
- a sharp strategist who understands the market
- a copywriter who knows what prospects really hesitate over
- a team that has seen the sales conversations before

It should not read like:
- a framework worksheet pasted into prose
- a motivational post
- a translation
- a generic AI summary

## 16. Non-Negotiables
- specificity beats abstraction
- proof beats hype
- structure beats improvisation
- angle testing beats random rewrites
- real customer language beats agency jargon
- native phrasing beats literal translation
- one strong argument beats five weak promises
